They streamed themselves the whole time (keeping a stream going, more or less, for 336 hours straight) and raising CASSSSSSSH for Ukrainian charity [[Road to Relief|https://www.roadtorelief.org/]] (who also receive 100% of the profits from donation sales of this game) in the process. In early 2023, a friend of mine, who I only knew by his Final Fantasy 14 handle, announced that he would be temporarily leaving our raid group because he had signed up to work with Doctors Without Borders in Ukraine. While I was, of course, worried for him, I had a deep admiration for what he was doing. The full scale Russian invasion of Ukraine on February 24th, 2022 had affected me like no global event had effected me before. Russian history was something I had (completely coincidentally) been spending a good deal of time reading for over a year before the invasion and when the war started I wanted to do something useful with that insight. Despite spending the next six months writing [[A Chinese Room|https://milomesdag.itch.io/a-chinese-room]], an alternate history exploring a Russia which won the war in the matter of weeks it appeared like it would immediately following the invasion, I didn’t feel like I had really achieved that. That is why, when my friend returned from Ukraine and had a chance to spend some time with his old raid group again, I wanted to know as much about what he had done out there as possible. In short, he had spent some time volunteering in Lviv, had then moved to Kyiv, and, still unsatisfied, had joined up with a small NGO called [[Road to Relief|https://www.roadtorelief.org/]], who operate much closer to the very front lines than most other international organisations and NGOs are able/willing to go. He told me stories of visiting villages where not a single pane of glass hadn’t been shattered by artillery fire, of pulling soldiers out of trenches, and of the incredible kindness of the Ukrainian people. He spoke about his own problems, which I’m not going to share here, but even as he spoke of hell, and his journey through it, it was clear just how meaningful the experience had been to him on a personal level. It was clear that, even though he had only spent two weeks in the country, and only some of that actually at the front, he would never be the same person again. There was a part of me that wanted that. To have found something so meaningful that nothing else in life would ever quite feel ‘right’ again. To cut a long story short, on my friend’s, who had finally insisted I call him ‘Nick’ by this point, advice, I had settled on the idea of travelling to Lviv and volunteering with [[Frontline Kitchen|https://www.volunteeringukraine.com/volunteer-opportunuties/front-line-kitchen]]. I had all the travel plans organised in my head, but whenever I thought about actually booking them, actually travelling by myself (something I, a self diagnosed Agoraphobe, haven’t really ever done) to a warzone, I felt myself begin to panic. I had more than one sleepless night just considering it. So I asked a friend, Jason Ebblewhite, to go with me. At first he was enthusiastic, but as he (and I) looked more and more into the details, the worries intensified. His mother did not want him to go and, once she found out about the government’s strict advice against travelling to Ukraine and the lack of any embassy or consulate in the entire country, my mother didn’t either. And so we decided we wouldn’t. That I felt an immediate surge of relief was probably an indication that it was the right idea. “We could do a charity stream instead,” Jason joked as we got ready to recover from our relieved disappointment with a burger. I laughed, but I spent the next quarter of an hour not being able to get it out of my head. I’d done a fundraising stream before and I’d done writing streams before. Jason was a programmer and we have been working on a game together for the last few years (a [[demo|https://pretentiousgoldfish.itch.io/the-wolf-and-wheel]] for which we released last year…). One of my main worries in going to Ukraine was that I had no skills I could actually use there. I was terrified that I’d go to a war zone and just be in the way. But writing is something I like to think I’m passable at, as is streaming. I was surprised when I brought the idea back up over supper and Jason was instantly on board. His enthusiasm was infectious and we had the whole thing planned out by the time we got back to my flat. We had been planning to spend two weeks in Lviv, so instead we’d just spend two weeks doing a game-making stream. And when I say two weeks, I mean two weeks. The original plan was to have at least one of us on camera more or less every single minute for 336 hours straight. We invited the third member of our game making trio (of Pretentious Goldfish Games), illustrator [[Angus Barker|https://www.instagram.com/anguspbarker.illustration/]] to join us whenever he could and, again to my surprise, he said yes. And so … so we did it. There were some hiccups along the way. The software I had wanted to use to collect donations (of course we were supporting Road to Relief) ended up not being quite as free as it had advertised as being and it became clear after just three days that streaming for 24 hours a day wasn’t conducive to good content, so the hours of 4am-8am were cut, but otherwise we did what we set out to be and raised AMOUNT OF CASH MONEY HOT DOLLAR HERE. Highlights included making borsch (something neither of us had even eaten before), getting absolutely destroyed in Smash Bros as part of an epic Jason vs Milo game showdown and WALK MAYBE ;). But the most important moments, for me, where the two times we got to video call with Nick and talk to him about Road to Relief, his life back in Ukraine and the all the wonderful people he’s met and all the amazing things he’s done (as well as all of the horror he’s seen).
